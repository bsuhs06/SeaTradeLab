How it works

What is AIS and why does it matter?+
AIS (Automatic Identification System) is a mandatory tracking system for commercial vessels. Ships broadcast their identity, position, speed, course, and destination via VHF radio. The collector pulls this data from the Finnish Digitraffic Maritime API, which covers the Baltic Sea, Gulf of Finland, and surrounding waters, polling every 10 minutes to build a picture of vessel movements.

Under international law (IMO SOLAS), all ships over 300 gross tons on international voyages must transmit AIS. Turning off AIS is a red flag that often indicates sanctions evasion, illicit transfers, or smuggling.
How does Ship-to-Ship (STS) transfer detection work?+
The STS detector scans position data to find pairs of vessels that:
  • Come within close proximity (default: 500 meters, configurable)
  • Both slow down significantly (speed under 3 knots, configurable)
  • Remain close for a sustained period (default: 15+ minutes)
The algorithm uses a KD-tree spatial index for efficient proximity searching, then computes precise geodesic distances for candidate pairs. Events are grouped across consecutive time windows and assigned confidence scores:
  • HIGH Duration 60+ minutes — very likely a transfer
  • MEDIUM Duration 15-60 minutes — probable transfer
  • LOW Duration under 15 minutes — possible brief encounter
All parameters (distance threshold, speed threshold, minimum duration) are configurable via the analytics CLI.
What are "dark vessels" and AIS gaps?+
A "dark vessel" is one that has stopped transmitting AIS data. The gap detector tracks this by comparing each vessel's last known position timestamp against the current time. Vessels are flagged as dark when their AIS gap exceeds a configurable threshold (default: 6 hours).

It also detects historical AIS gaps — periods within a vessel's track where no position reports were received for an extended time. Two vessels going dark in proximity is a strong indicator of a covert ship-to-ship transfer.

Important caveat: the Finnish Digitraffic API has regional coverage. A vessel sailing out of the Baltic may appear to "go dark" simply because it left the coverage area.
How does Russian port visit detection work?+
The port visit detector monitors vessel positions near key Russian ports in its coverage area:
  • Primorsk — Major crude oil terminal (Gulf of Finland)
  • Ust-Luga — Oil, gas, and coal terminal (Gulf of Finland)
  • Vysotsk — Oil terminal near Vyborg
  • St. Petersburg — Russia's largest Baltic commercial port
  • Kaliningrad — Russian exclave port
The system identifies each vessel's flag state from its MMSI prefix (Maritime ID digits assigned by ITU). Non-Russian vessels visiting these ports are particularly interesting for sanctions monitoring, as they may be loading sanctioned Russian oil or cargo.

A visit is recorded when a vessel remains within the port boundary (typically 4-8 km radius) for at least 30 minutes. Duration, vessel type, and flag state are all tracked.
What is a "ghost fleet" / dark fleet?+
The "dark fleet" or "shadow fleet" refers to a growing network of aging tankers used to circumvent international sanctions on Russian oil exports. These vessels often:
  • Operate under flags of convenience (Cameroon, Gabon, Palau, etc.)
  • Turn off AIS transponders during transfers
  • Conduct ship-to-ship transfers in open water to obscure oil origins
  • Lack proper insurance (P&I club coverage)
  • Use shell companies to hide ownership
This tracker focuses on detecting the behavioral signatures of these operations: proximity events, AIS gaps, port visits, and unusual movement patterns near known transfer zones.
How does the time slider work?+
The time slider on the map page lets you view historical vessel positions at any point in time. When you drag the slider, it queries the database for each vessel's most recent position at or before the selected time (within a 1-hour window). This effectively replays vessel movements hour by hour.

Use the play button to animate the map, watching vessels move across the Baltic. The slider range spans from the earliest collected data to the current time.
What configurable parameters are available?+
The detection algorithms accept several tunable parameters:

--distance STS proximity threshold in meters (default: 500m)
--speed Maximum SOG to count as "loitering" in knots (default: 3.0 kn)
--min-duration Minimum encounter duration in minutes (default: 15 min)
--gap-hours Minimum AIS gap to flag in hours (default: 2.0h)
--hours How far back to scan in hours (default: 24h)
--russian-ports Scan for vessels visiting Russian ports

On the map page, the AIS staleness filter lets you dynamically show/hide vessels based on how recently they reported.
Data source and coverage area+
Data comes from the Finnish Digitraffic Maritime API, a free, public API provided by the Finnish Transport Infrastructure Agency. Coverage includes:
  • Gulf of Finland
  • Baltic Sea (northern and central)
  • Gulf of Bothnia
  • Approaches to St. Petersburg, Helsinki, Tallinn, Stockholm
Key Russian ports in coverage: Primorsk, Ust-Luga, Vysotsk, St. Petersburg. Other monitored ports: Helsinki, Tallinn/Muuga, Hamina-Kotka, Porvoo/Kilpilahti, Turku, Gdansk.

Data is polled every 10 minutes. Total positions in database grow by approximately 2.5 million per day.
